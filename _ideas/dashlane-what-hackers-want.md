# What a Hacker Actually Wants From You

## The Idea
Most people imagine hackers targeting banks or governments. In reality, the most valuable thing a hacker can get from an ordinary person is their password manager database — one file that unlocks their entire digital life. Understanding what's actually in that file changes how you think about online security.

## Why It's Interesting
It reframes the threat from abstract ("someone might hack me") to concrete ("here's exactly what they'd find and what they'd do with it"). A password manager export contains not just passwords but credit card numbers, banking details, personal identification, security questions, PINs, and recovery codes. It's a complete dossier. Seeing it laid out plainly makes the stakes real without being alarmist.

## Key Points to Cover
- What's actually in a password manager vault: credentials, payment info, personal data, secure notes
- Why this is more valuable than just stealing one password
- What attackers do with stolen credentials: account takeovers, financial fraud, identity theft
- The "credential stuffing" technique: trying stolen username/password pairs across hundreds of other sites automatically
- Why the master password is so critical — and what "encrypted vault" actually protects against
