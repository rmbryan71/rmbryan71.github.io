# What Gets Thrown Away When You Make an MP3

## The Idea
An MP3 file of a song sounds almost identical to the original CD — but it's roughly 10 times smaller. That size reduction comes from permanently discarding information that humans are unlikely to notice is missing. Understanding what gets thrown away, and how that decision is made, is one of the more fascinating stories in the history of digital technology.

## Why It's Interesting
It involves both psychology and mathematics. The MP3 format is built on a model of human hearing — which frequencies we notice, which we don't, which sounds get masked by other sounds. The algorithm throws away the parts your brain was probably going to ignore anyway. That's a deeply human-centered piece of engineering, and it reveals something true about how perception works.

## Key Points to Cover
- What "lossy" compression means: data is permanently removed to reduce file size
- The psychoacoustic model: what the human ear actually perceives vs. ignores
- Specific examples: sounds behind louder sounds, very high frequencies, very quiet sounds in busy passages
- What "lossless" compression does instead: reorganizes data without removing any
- Why lossless matters for archiving: you can always make a lossy copy from lossless, but you can never recover what lossy threw away
