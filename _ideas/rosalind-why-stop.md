# Why I Stopped at 70 Problems (Out of 300)

## The Idea
Project Rosalind has over 300 problems. After solving 70 and writing a blog post about each one, the project ended — not because it was finished, but because it felt done. Understanding why people stop before completion, and what "done" really means in a learning project, is more interesting than the problems themselves.

## Why It's Interesting
Most ambitious projects aren't abandoned — they're declared complete at a natural stopping point that's short of the original goal. That's not failure; it's a realistic response to diminishing returns, shifting priorities, and the recognition that the most valuable learning has already happened. But we rarely talk about those decisions honestly. This is an honest account of one.

## Key Points to Cover
- The setup: 300+ problems, 70 solved, wrap-up post written
- What changed: later problems require increasingly specialized biological domain knowledge that wasn't the learning goal
- Diminishing returns on the specific skill being developed (Python problem-solving) vs. growing cost (biology domain expertise)
- The difference between stopping and quitting: a conscious decision vs. drift
- What the project actually accomplished — independent of the number of problems solved
