# Why Thinking Harder Beats Computing Faster

## The Idea
Project Euler problems are deliberately designed so that brute force — trying every possible answer — doesn't work within a reasonable time limit. The only way through is mathematical insight. This makes them a perfect illustration of a principle that applies far beyond math: throwing more resources at a problem is rarely the best solution.

## Why It's Interesting
We live in an era where computing power is cheap and growing. The temptation is always to solve harder problems by adding more computing power. But many problems have an inherent structure that makes smarter approaches possible — and those approaches aren't just faster, they're often more elegant, more general, and more revealing about what the problem actually is. Project Euler teaches that instinct.

## Key Points to Cover
- What brute force means: trying every possibility until you find the answer
- Why it often works for small problems but fails for large ones (exponential growth)
- A concrete example: checking every fraction under one million vs. summing a mathematical series
- The payoff of insight: not just speed, but understanding why the answer is what it is
- Where this principle shows up outside math: business strategy, scientific research, system design
