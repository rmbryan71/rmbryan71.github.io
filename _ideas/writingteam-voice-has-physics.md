# Your Writing Voice Has Measurable Physics

## The Idea
Most people think of writing voice as something ineffable — you either have it or you don't, and it can't really be described. But it turns out voice is measurable: average sentence length, paragraph structure, how often you ask questions, which words you return to, how you open sentences. These patterns form a kind of fingerprint. And once you can see the fingerprint, you can work with it deliberately.

## Why It's Interesting
It reframes a subjective, mysterious thing as something concrete and analyzable — without making it feel mechanical. The measurement doesn't flatten voice; it reveals structure that was always there. Understanding your own patterns lets you apply them intentionally, recognize when you've drifted from them, and describe them to collaborators (human or AI) in a way that produces consistent results.

## Key Points to Cover
- What makes voice: sentence length, paragraph habits, question frequency, vocabulary choices, sentence openings
- How these can be measured: word counts, statistical analysis, pattern detection across many samples
- What the numbers reveal: average sentence length of 12 words reads differently from 22 words — and both can be "good"
- What measurement misses: the reasoning behind the choices, the tone that emerges from specific word combinations
- Why this matters: knowing your patterns lets you replicate them deliberately, not just accidentally
