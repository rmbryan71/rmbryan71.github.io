# Why We Save Things

## The Idea
Someone ripped an independent musician's album to lossless audio with cryptographic verification and preserved it in two formats. The album isn't rare. The artist isn't famous. Nobody asked. It's a small act of preservation for its own sake — and it points to something true about why humans keep things.

## Why It's Interesting
Preservation is a deeply human impulse. We save letters, photographs, recipes, recordings — not because they're objectively important, but because they're meaningful to us and we don't want them to disappear. Digital preservation borrows the tools of professional archivists (checksums, lossless formats, format redundancy) and applies them to personal acts of keeping. Understanding why that impulse exists is more interesting than the technical mechanics.

## Key Points to Cover
- The human impulse: we save things that matter to us, regardless of their objective importance
- How personal archives differ from institutional archives: what gets saved is determined by personal attachment, not cultural significance
- The digital tools that make personal preservation possible: lossless ripping, checksums, redundant storage
- What we're deciding when we save something: that it deserves to continue existing
- What gets lost when we don't: not just the object, but the experience of encountering it
