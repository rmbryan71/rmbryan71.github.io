# Why Mathematicians Are Obsessed with Prime Numbers

## The Idea
Prime numbers — numbers divisible only by themselves and one — show up in more Project Euler problems than any other concept. They're the atoms of arithmetic: every number is built from them, and yet their distribution seems almost random. That combination of fundamental importance and unpredictable behavior has captivated mathematicians for thousands of years.

## Why It's Interesting
Primes aren't just abstract — they're the foundation of internet security. Every time you make a purchase online, a prime number is protecting your credit card. Understanding what primes are, why they matter, and why they're so hard to work with connects a centuries-old mathematical puzzle to something happening on your phone right now.

## Key Points to Cover
- What a prime number is (simple definition)
- Why they're the "atoms" of arithmetic: every number factors into primes uniquely
- The distribution problem: primes become less frequent as numbers grow, but never stop appearing
- Why they're useful for cryptography: multiplying two large primes is easy; figuring out the original primes from the product is (currently) impossibly hard
- Why they keep appearing in computational problems: efficiency and factorization
