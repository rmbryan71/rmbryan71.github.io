# When Is It Okay to Look at Someone Else's Solution?

## The Idea
The hardest judgment call in independent learning isn't "how do I solve this?" — it's "have I struggled long enough to earn looking at the answer?" After 25 hours on a single problem with no progress, looking at someone else's code and then rewriting it in your own way produced genuine understanding. But looking too early teaches nothing. The question of when to look is more interesting than it appears.

## Why It's Interesting
It's a question that applies to learning anything: programming, music, language, chess. Independent struggle builds problem-solving instincts. Studying others' solutions builds knowledge of what's possible. Both are necessary. The hard part is knowing which you need at any given moment — and resisting the guilt that makes people either look too early (avoiding the struggle) or too late (wasting time).

## Key Points to Cover
- The specific case: 25 hours on Sorting by Reversals with no original progress
- What looking at another solution provided: not the answer, but the conceptual approach that made the answer possible
- The difference between copying and learning: reading someone's solution, closing it, and rewriting from understanding
- When to look early: when you don't have the prerequisite knowledge (no shame in that)
- When to push longer: when you have the tools and just need to find the connection
