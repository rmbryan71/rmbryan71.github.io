# What Makes an Acoustic Recording Sound Like the Room

## The Idea
An acoustic recording — one microphone, one guitar, no processing — captures something a produced studio album can't: the sound of a specific person in a specific room at a specific moment. What makes that kind of recording distinctive, and what choices go into capturing it well, is a story about the relationship between space, sound, and technology.

## Why It's Interesting
Most people think of recording quality in terms of production value — more instrumentation, cleaner sound, bigger budget. But acoustic minimalism is its own discipline, with its own aesthetic goals and technical demands. A well-made acoustic recording is intimate in a way that produced music rarely is. Understanding what goes into making one changes how you hear it.

## Key Points to Cover
- What "acoustic" recording means: minimal or no electronic processing, capturing the natural sound
- What a microphone actually captures: not just the instrument, but the room's reflections, ambient noise, the player's breathing
- The tradeoffs: intimacy and authenticity vs. control and polish
- What "bit-perfect" archiving preserves: every nuance of the original recording, including the room
- Why lossless formats matter more for acoustic recordings than produced ones: there's less to mask imperfections in what's thrown away
