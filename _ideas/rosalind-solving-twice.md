# What It's Like to Solve a Problem You've Already Solved and Completely Forgotten

## The Idea
Project Rosalind showed that I'd solved a problem in 2018 and completely forgotten having done it by 2025. Not just forgotten the solution — forgotten the experience of solving it. What does that say about what we actually retain from learning, and what learning is really for?

## Why It's Interesting
It punctures the idea that learning is primarily about retaining information. If you solve a hard problem and forget the solution, did you learn anything? The answer seems to be yes — the problem-solving instincts, the pattern recognition, the tolerance for struggle — those persist even when the specific solution doesn't. That reframe has implications for how we think about education, memory, and what expertise actually is.

## Key Points to Cover
- The discovery: Project Rosalind's records showed a problem solved in 2018, with no memory of it
- What was retained: not the solution, but apparently the capability to approach the problem type
- What was lost: the specific path, the specific code, the specific aha moment
- Why this is actually reassuring: you don't need to remember everything you've learned to have been changed by it
- The implication: learning changes how you think, not just what you know
