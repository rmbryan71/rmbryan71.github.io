# Did Writing About It Make Me Better at Solving It?

## The Idea
Every Project Rosalind post required restating the problem in plain language before attempting a solution. That constraint — explain it before you solve it — turns out to be one of the most effective learning strategies in existence. Looking back across 70 problems, the act of writing changed how the problems got solved.

## Why It's Interesting
The relationship between writing and thinking is well-established in education research but underappreciated in practice. Writing forces clarity. You can hold a vague understanding in your head indefinitely; you can't write around it. The Rosalind blog is an accidental experiment in this — one person solving 70 problems, writing about each one, and implicitly testing whether documentation changes the quality of thinking.

## Key Points to Cover
- The restating-the-problem practice: what it is and why it was the starting point for every post
- What happens when you have to explain something: you discover what you don't actually understand
- Specific examples from the posts: moments where writing forced a clarification that unlocked the solution
- The "explain to a friend" technique: why teaching something is the deepest form of learning
- The takeaway: writing isn't just a way to record thinking — it's a way to do thinking
