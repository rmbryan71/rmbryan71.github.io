# One Piece of Writing, Five Different Places

## The Idea
A well-written essay contains the material for a newsletter, three social posts, a short summary, and a talk introduction. Most writers treat each of these as separate projects and write them from scratch. Repurposing — adapting existing content for different formats — is a skill, and doing it well requires understanding both the original piece and the specific demands of each destination format.

## Why It's Interesting
Content repurposing is one of the most underutilized productivity strategies in writing. The ideas are already there; the work is the format conversion. Understanding what each format actually needs — not just "make it shorter," but what a newsletter introduction does vs. what a social post does — makes repurposing systematic rather than haphazard.

## Key Points to Cover
- What repurposing actually means: not copying and pasting, but adapting for a new format's specific needs
- What different formats need: newsletters (context + payoff), social posts (hook + single idea), summaries (conclusion first)
- What stays the same across formats: the core idea, the voice, the supporting evidence
- What changes: length, structure, assumed prior knowledge, call to action
- How to evaluate a repurposed piece: does it feel native to the format, or like a shrunken essay?
