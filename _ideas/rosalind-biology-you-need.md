# How Much Biology Do You Actually Need to Solve Bioinformatics Problems?

## The Idea
Bioinformatics sounds like it requires a biology degree. It doesn't — at least not for most Project Rosalind problems. The surprising discovery: most problems are fundamentally about algorithms that happen to use biological data as their input. You need enough biology to understand the problem statement, but the actual solution is usually pure programming and mathematics.

## Why It's Interesting
It challenges the assumption that domain expertise is a prerequisite for entry. Many fields feel gated by jargon and background knowledge that turns out to be much thinner than it appears once you start working. Understanding where the knowledge barrier actually sits — as opposed to where it appears to sit — is useful for anyone trying to enter a new field.

## Key Points to Cover
- The intimidating surface: DNA strings, protein sequences, phylogenetic trees — sounds like biology class
- The actual requirement: understanding what the biological terms mean for the purposes of this problem, not deep biological knowledge
- Specific example: the De Bruijn graph problem requires understanding graph traversal, not genome assembly biology — the biology is just context
- Where biology actually matters: a few problems genuinely require understanding evolutionary relationships or biochemical processes
- The takeaway: barriers to entry in technical fields are often smaller than they appear
