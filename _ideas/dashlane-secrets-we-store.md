# The Strange Things We Trust Our Password Managers With

## The Idea
Password managers were invented for passwords. But people use them for everything: PIN numbers, license plates, VINs, library card numbers, door codes, passport numbers, frequent flyer numbers, software license keys. The "secure notes" section of a password vault is a window into human psychology — we put anything we need to remember but can't afford to lose.

## Why It's Interesting
It reveals something true about how people actually behave with technology vs. how they're supposed to behave. No security expert recommended storing your car's VIN in your password manager. But humans need a trusted, accessible place for anything important, and the password manager became that place by being the most trustworthy thing in the digital toolkit. That's a story about how tools get adopted for uses nobody designed them for.

## Key Points to Cover
- What password managers were designed for vs. what people actually use them for
- Examples of non-password data people store: PINs, codes, account numbers, identity documents
- Why the "secure notes" feature exists and why it became so important
- The logic behind it: if I trust this with my bank password, I trust it with my passport number
- The risk: all of this data becomes part of the single point of failure
