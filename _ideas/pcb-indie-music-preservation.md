# The Albums No One Is Saving But You

## The Idea
When a major label artist's album gets released, dozens of people archive it: libraries, streaming services, music databases, fan sites. When an independent musician self-releases an album, almost none of that happens. The only copies might be the ones the artist has, and the ones a few devoted listeners made. This is the preservation gap that leaves most independent music one hard drive failure away from being lost forever.

## Why It's Interesting
It raises questions about what cultural memory we're choosing to preserve and what we're letting disappear. Major label releases have institutional backing. Independent art — the most personal, often the most honest — has almost none. Personal archiving is a form of cultural stewardship that most people don't think of it as, but it is.

## Key Points to Cover
- The contrast: major releases vs. independent releases and their preservation odds
- What "not in the AccurateRip database" means: the album is obscure enough that no reference checksum exists — you can't verify your copy against anyone else's
- The preservation gap: streaming services don't archive; they license
- What listeners can do: rip what you own, store it well, keep backups
- The broader question: what independent art is being lost right now because nobody is systematically saving it?
