# Why the Same Writer Sounds Different in an Email, an Essay, and a Tweet

## The Idea
Your writing voice isn't one thing — it's a family of related styles that share core patterns but adapt to different contexts. The voice in a personal essay is the same person as the voice in a work email, but the sentence length, formality, and tone shift significantly. Understanding those variants, and being able to describe them, is what makes consistent writing possible across formats.

## Why It's Interesting
Most people don't think of their own writing as having deliberate format variants — they just write differently in different contexts without knowing why. Making those differences explicit reveals something about how writing actually works: it's always a negotiation between your natural patterns and the expectations of the format. Seeing that clearly helps you write more intentionally and helps collaborators (or AI tools) get the right register on the first try.

## Key Points to Cover
- The observation: the same person sounds noticeably different in different formats
- What changes: formality, sentence length, question frequency, use of humor
- What stays the same: underlying vocabulary choices, narrative instincts, values expressed
- Why this matters for AI assistance: specifying "email voice" vs. "essay voice" produces much better results
- The practical use: a voice profile that documents variants, not just a single "voice"
