# Your CDs Are Dying

## The Idea
Compact discs were supposed to last forever. The original marketing promised "perfect sound, forever." In reality, CDs degrade — sometimes within 20 years, sometimes within 50, depending on manufacturing quality and storage conditions. The physical medium that holds the music starts to fail, and when it does, the recording is gone. Most people have no idea this is happening to their collections right now.

## Why It's Interesting
It punctures a persistent myth about digital permanence. People assume digital means durable — that once something is encoded as ones and zeros, it's safe. But the physical media that stores those ones and zeros is as vulnerable to time as vinyl or magnetic tape. Understanding CD degradation is a window into the broader truth that no storage medium is permanent, and the only protection is active preservation.

## Key Points to Cover
- The original promise: "perfect sound, forever" — and why people believed it
- How CDs actually store data: microscopic pits in a polycarbonate disc, read by a laser
- What degrades: the reflective layer oxidizes, the lacquer coating cracks, the disc warps
- The timeline: "disc rot" can begin in as little as 10 years with poor manufacturing; most discs are safe for 25-100 years
- What you should do about it: rip to lossless formats now, before it's too late
