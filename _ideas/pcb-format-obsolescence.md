# What Happens When the Hardware Disappears

## The Idea
In 1985, nobody thought CD players would ever be hard to find. In 2025, finding a working CD player is an errand. The same pattern has played out with VHS players, floppy disk drives, cassette decks, and LaserDisc players. Every physical format eventually loses its hardware ecosystem — and when it does, the content becomes inaccessible unless it was migrated in time.

## Why It's Interesting
It's a recurring pattern in technology history with no signs of stopping. The lesson isn't specific to CDs — it's about the relationship between content and the infrastructure required to access it. Understanding that pattern makes it easier to think ahead about what you're storing and whether you'll still be able to access it in twenty years.

## Key Points to Cover
- The pattern: new format emerges → becomes dominant → hardware becomes scarce → content becomes inaccessible
- Historical examples: 8-track, Betamax, VHS, LaserDisc, Zip drives, floppy disks, CDs
- The current threats: physical CDs, BluRay, even some streaming-only content
- The migration imperative: you have to move content to new formats before the old ones become inaccessible
- What "format agnostic" preservation means: lossless audio files that can be played by anything, now and in the future
