# Why 3,900 People Solved Problem 40 and 900 Solved Problem 65

## The Idea
Project Rosalind shows how many people have solved each problem. The early problems have thousands of solvers. The later ones have hundreds. That drop-off isn't gradual — it's a cliff. Understanding where that cliff is and why it exists tells you something important about how difficulty works, and about where most learners give up.

## Why It's Interesting
The drop-off in solvers is a map of where people stop. Most educational experiences have similar hidden cliff points — places where the difficulty jumps beyond what most people can bridge without additional support. Knowing where those points are, and why, is useful for anyone designing a learning experience, teaching someone, or trying to push through a plateau themselves.

## Key Points to Cover
- The data: solver counts for early problems (3,000+) vs. later problems (900-1,600)
- What the drop-off represents: the point where domain knowledge becomes load-bearing
- The specific cliff: later problems require graph theory, dynamic programming, combinatorics — algorithms that need to be actively learned, not just applied
- Why some people make it past the cliff: patience, prerequisite knowledge, and the willingness to spend 25 hours on a single problem
- The broader lesson: every discipline has these plateaus, and most people stop just before they'd break through
