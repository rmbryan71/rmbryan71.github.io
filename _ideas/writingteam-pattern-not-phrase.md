# Voice Lives in Patterns, Not in Signature Phrases

## The Idea
When people try to imitate a writer's voice, they copy the memorable phrases — the distinctive vocabulary, the famous sentences. But that's not where voice lives. Voice lives in patterns: sentence rhythm, how paragraphs build, when to use a question vs. a statement, how long to sit with an idea before moving on. Copy the phrases and you get parody. Copy the patterns and you get the voice.

## Why It's Interesting
It's a counterintuitive insight about how style actually works — and it matters as much for human editors and ghostwriters as for AI tools. The implication is that to capture someone's voice, you need to study their patterns systematically, not just read them impressionistically. That's a different kind of attention than most readers bring to writing.

## Key Points to Cover
- The common mistake: mimicking distinctive phrases or vocabulary
- Why it produces parody: the phrases are the surface, not the structure
- Where voice actually lives: sentence rhythm, paragraph architecture, the ratio of statement to question
- How to study patterns: read many samples looking for structure, not content
- The practical implication: a voice guide based on patterns is more useful than a list of favorite phrases
