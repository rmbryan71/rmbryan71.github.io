# Why Not Knowing What Your Opponent Has Changes Everything

## The Idea
Most card games let everyone see everything that's happening. This game has a Cloaking card that hides your actions from other players. That one addition changes the entire strategic landscape — suddenly you're not just playing the game, you're making decisions under genuine uncertainty. It turns out, hidden information is one of the most powerful design tools in any competitive game.

## Why It's Interesting
Information asymmetry — when one person knows something the other doesn't — shows up everywhere: poker, negotiations, job offers, medical diagnoses. Games that include it are more stressful, more interesting, and more realistic than games that don't. Understanding why hidden information creates better games helps explain why it shows up everywhere in life too.

## Key Points to Cover
- What "perfect information" games look like (chess: both players see everything)
- What hidden information changes: you have to guess, plan for multiple scenarios, and sometimes bluff
- How Cloaking works in the game: your play isn't visible to opponents
- The counter-play it creates: if you don't know what someone played, you have to change how you evaluate your own options
- Real-world parallels: negotiating a salary when you don't know the employer's budget
