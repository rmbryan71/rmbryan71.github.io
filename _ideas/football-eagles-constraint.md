# Why Starting with One Team Makes a Better Game

## The Idea
This fantasy football system starts with just the Philadelphia Eagles roster. Not all 32 NFL teams — one. That single constraint changes everything about how the game is played, who plays it, and what knowledge actually matters. It's the same design insight behind EBL, and it works for the same reasons: restriction creates depth.

## Why It's Interesting
It's a counter-intuitive design principle with wide application. More options seem better until they aren't — until the choices become overwhelming, the competition becomes generic, and the community fractures across too many interests. Restricting to one team creates a shared reference point, forces genuine roster knowledge, and makes every player on the depth chart potentially meaningful. The same principle works in product design, education, and creative constraints.

## Key Points to Cover
- The traditional fantasy problem: 32 teams, hundreds of players, impossible to know everyone well
- What one-team restriction does: forces deep knowledge of a real roster
- The community effect: all players follow the same games, share the same emotional stakes
- The strategic effect: the entire depth chart matters — a backup suddenly matters
- The scale plan: start with one team as a proof of concept, then expand to all 32
