# The One Password Paradox

## The Idea
Password managers solve an impossible problem — remembering 100+ unique, strong passwords — by asking you to remember exactly one. That's genuinely clever. But it also means one forgotten master password, or one successful attack, unlocks everything at once. How did we get here, and is this actually a good tradeoff?

## Why It's Interesting
It's a clean, relatable example of a tradeoff that shows up everywhere: solving a hard problem by concentrating risk instead of eliminating it. The average person has more than 80 online accounts. No one can remember 80 strong, unique passwords. The password manager solution is brilliant and unsettling at the same time — and most people use one without thinking carefully about what they've agreed to.

## Key Points to Cover
- The original problem: password reuse is the #1 cause of account breaches
- What a password manager actually does: one encrypted vault, one master password to open it
- The tradeoff: convenience and security on one side, single point of failure on the other
- What "encrypted vault" means in plain terms — why the company can't see your passwords
- What happens if you forget the master password (usually: you're locked out forever)
